The Design of Silence: What Software Gets Wrong About Restraint

Every major platform now offers a "minimal" mode. Almost none of them are actually minimal. On the difference between removing things and making space.

AuthorKira Lindqvist
PhotographsElsa Mehta
PublishedMay 2026
Reading time12 minutes

There is a Zen koan about the uses of emptiness. A wheel has spokes, but it is the hole in the hub that makes it useful. A vessel has walls, but it is the hollow inside that makes it useful. Software design rarely thinks about the hollow.

When Dieter Rams reduced the Braun T3 pocket radio to a small rectangle with a single dial, he was not performing minimalism as aesthetic. He was solving a problem about cognitive load. The fewer decisions a user must make about the object itself, the more of their attention is available for what they actually want to do.

"The fewer decisions a user must make about the object itself, the more of their attention is available for what they actually want to do."

This principle has been badly misunderstood by contemporary software design. "Minimal" has become an aesthetic descriptor — flat icons, neutral grays, generous padding — rather than a functional one. The result is interfaces that look restrained and feel cluttered. The visual noise has been removed; the cognitive noise has not.

The confetti of micro-interactions

Open any contemporary productivity app — the flagship ones, the ones written about in design press — and interact with it for thirty minutes. Count how many times you receive a visual reward you did not ask for. A green checkmark animation. A confetti burst when you complete a task. An inline celebration when you send a message.

These animations are not accidents. They are intentional dopamine triggers, borrowed from the vocabulary of games. The design team behind each of them can almost certainly point to A/B test data showing increased engagement. And yet they represent a fundamental failure of restraint — not because they are ugly, but because they are unsolicited.

What restraint actually requires

The discipline of restraint is harder than the discipline of simplicity. Simplicity asks: what can we remove? Restraint asks: what should we withhold even though we could offer it?

The distinction matters because most design teams are better equipped for simplicity. The tooling for simplicity is straightforward — wireframes, user testing, design audits. The tooling for restraint is more philosophical. It requires asking whose attention you are competing for, and why.
